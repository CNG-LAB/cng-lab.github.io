Our questions & how and why we try to answer them



We aim to understand why the social environment is so important for human mental health from a neurobiological perspective. Within this context we are particularly interested to understand how genetic factors 'build' the brain to make it interact with other humans, how this interplay occurs along phylogeny/ontogeny, and how social-environmental factors interact with this neurogenetic scaffold.

In our long-term aim to tackle this question, we integrate approaches along the bio-psycho-social continuum, with a focus on neuroimaging. Specifically; we want to understand how genetic factors systematically shape neuroanatomy and organize the human brain, enabling it to suppport human (social) cognition. To reach this goal, we combine approaches from behavioral genetics, neuroimaging, neuroanatomy, phylogenetic models, and transcriptomics. Ultimately, our maps may be applied to clinical context, helping to understand the neurogenetic basis of brain disorders. Moreover, we want to understand how socio-environmental factors shape brain structure, function and associated behaviors. To answer this question, we use cross-sectional, longitudinal, and developmental multimodal neuroimaging and behavioral datasets to study the impact of social environment and behavior on brain and behavior. Ultimately, outcomes of our work may help to develop targeted interventions nurturing social behavior to promote mental health.

Manifolds in humans and macaques

We have been able to establish a link between the macroscale cortical axes recounting the genetic correlation between brain regions and phylogenetic models of cortical expansion and the dual origin model in humans and macaques. Such manifolds may provide a coordinate system along which brain development and function can be understood.

Microstructure and function

How does a relatively stable structure give rise to flexible (social) cognition? Contrary to primary areas, transmodal areas may show an 'uncoupling' of structure and function. We are currently investigating the interrelationship of structure and function, and its varying across the neocortex in humans and non-human primates.

Models of brain disorder

We are integrating our models of brain organization with maps of disorder impact to further understand how brain disorder may systematically impact structure and function.

Analyses and toolboxes

Together with the mica-lab we have developed brainspace, a statistical and decoding toolbox in matlab and python. By co-developing various toolboxes we hope to contribute to robust and integrative neuroscience.
