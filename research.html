Our group integrates macro- and meso-scale neuroimaging measures of the human and non-human primate brain with behavioral measures and computational methods to provide an interdisciplinary account on the question how brain structure supports higher cognitive function, and its relation to brain disorder.

Our research (1) develops new tools and approaches to study brain structure and function, (2) characterizes phylogentic and ontogenetic processes that shape the human brain and (3) applies computational analyses to model meaningful variations within and between individuals







Findings and discoveries



Manifolds in humans and macaques

We have been able to establish a link between the macroscale cortical axes recounting the genetic correlation between brain regions and phylogenetic models of cortical expansion and the dual origin model in humans and macaques. Such manifolds may provide a coordinate system along which brain development and function can be understood.

Microstructure and function

How does a relatively stable structure give rise to flexible cognition? Contrary to primary areas, transmodal areas may show an 'uncoupling' of structure and function. We are currently investigating the interrelationship of structure and function, and its varying across the neocortex in humans and non-human primates.

Models of brain disorder

We are integrating our models of brain organization with maps of disorder impact to further understand how brain disorder may systematically impact structure and function.

Analyses and tools

Together with the mica-lab we have developed brainspace, a statistical and decoding toolbox in matlab and python. By co-developing various toolboxes we hope to contribute to robust and integrative neuroscience.
